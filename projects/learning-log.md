# Why do you want to become a developer? 

This is a great question honestly. I have been taking programming classes since 7th grade as a tech credit that I needed. 
Of course, I was interested in the idea, that is why I decided to do it. 
I have always been relatively "smart". 

In those classes, I never really felt like I was the best. 
I remember in 7th and 8th grade I was struggling to complete the tasks they had asked me to do. I was not the first one to get things done. 
Oftentimes, I was even the last. But, I liked the struggle. It was like trying to solve a puzzle. It is hard and complicated but also rewarding. 

As I grew up, I got in to more and more developing classes, even though I needed help to complete the tasks. 
I started working with two of my friends in a C++ Object Oriented programming class. Collectively, we were unstoppable. 
We worked very well together. I was usually needing help while the two of them were chugging right on through coding challenges and projects. 
I was not afraid to ask questions; both to the teacher and to my two peers. 

Oftentimes, this got me to where I needed to go. The "figured it out" stage. The "aha" moment on topics. 
There were even a few times where my question-asking helped me understand the new topic in a way that I could explain to my two peers and help them understand the topic better. Sometimes I was able to help them back. This created a great relationship. 

Since after highschool, I went to college for a degree in web development. I quickly fell behind the rest of the class and ended up dropping the class after about 2 weeks. 

I dropped out of college after about 1 semester. It was not for me. 

I attempted codecademy and ended up getting quite far in a "Full-stack" web developer self-guided lesson plan. 
I was feeling more and more comfortable with HTML and CSS. 

I ended up pursuing other interests as I never felt like I had enough knowledge learning by myself. 

Then, I attended HTMElephant - A web developing cohort led by my best friend and his coworker. 
Honestly, super underrated. 

My buddy is very enthusiastic about web development and has always encouraged me to pursue a career. That is why he allowed me to take his cohort free of charge for 8 months of the course. He offered wednesday nights and Saturday morning/afternoons, even up to and beyond the birth of his first child. 

We learned so much. We learned in such a short amount of time in each session. We had in class work with guided mentorship through two very knowledgable senior full-stack developers. 
We had slide shows, coding challenges, notes, and projects. We worked as teams, we worked alone, and we even had to present. 

By the end of it, I was working on a Full-stack website with full CRUD capability and I was pushing code that didn't break. 

For the first time, I had felt PROFICIENT. 
I felt like I was a real life developer. 
I felt like I was at a point I could see myself turning this in to a career. 

Life takes a turn, as it always does. 
I chased the wrong things and have no regrets. I was after a life involved with cars. I love cars. 
I was making good money and starting carving out a competitive career in the automotive detailing/restyling industry. 

Then you get humbled. 
You realize your knees can't last forever. 
You cant' sit or kneel on concrete for your whole life. 
You realize you could make way more money doing these things on your own, but you don't want to be the business, you want to be a part of the business. 

You realize that this industry, although very rewarding, is cyclical. 
The seasons come and go, you slow down in the winter and overwork yourself in the summer. 

There are no companies offering benefits. There are no health, dental, vision, or 401k plans offered to detailers. 
There's not even PTO. 

You're left working as hard as you can just to stay in the same place, and burning the candle out at both ends for a career that you can't do forever. 

I realized I needed a change. 

I like to stay occupied. I like to keep my mind muscles working. I like to stay stimulated by my work. I like to create things. I take pride in the things that I do and I deeply care about the people I do them for. 
I work well with people. I am a self starter. I am an influencer. I guide a group of guys to stay on task. I show men how to take pride in the work they provide. I show a deeper meaning behind the work that needs to be done so that you actually want to do it. 
I create a stable work environment. I am efficient. I cut out unnecessary time wasting tasks. My phone is in focus modes that block social media during certain hours and set automatically enforced time restrictions during "non-work" hours. I cut out repetitive motions and behaviors. I have a clean work environment. I do all these things to create a peak work environment so the brain can create and the body follows orders. I create things I care about that help people. I do what I love, and I do it passionately.

I am a developer. 

# Git Learning Plan

ChatGPT estimates that for about 30 minutes to 1 hour on worknights, 4 hours on my weekday off, and 2 hours on two weekend days, I will be able to become a full stack web developer that is job ready in 6-9 months. 

My first week is all about Git and the coding environment. 
I am supposed to get familiar with git and the process of creating a repo, cloning a repo, branching, merging, commits, etc. 

I am also learning the regular workflow associated with web development and how things are going to be handled on the job.
